Chapter 1: Introduction

1.1 Overview

During my time at PSU Phuket, I noticed a pretty consistent pattern: students regularly need equipment they don't actually own. A third-year student needs a DSLR camera for a media project but only for one weekend. A first-year engineering student needs a scientific calculator for exam week. A group putting on a campus event needs costumes, props, or audio equipment for just two days. For most students, buying these things outright makes no sense financially — it's expensive, and you end up with something you'll use once and then forget about.

The natural solution, at least the way it works now, is to borrow from friends. And that works fine until it doesn't. Items come back late. Items come back damaged with no clear record of when the damage happened. Sometimes items don't come back at all. There's no formal process, no deposit system, and no record anyone can refer to if something goes wrong. Most people just absorb the loss because confronting a classmate over a borrowed item feels awkward, and there's no evidence to point to anyway.

Some students have tried using Facebook groups or LINE group chats to do informal rentals or sales. These work better than nothing, but they have real problems. There's no verification that the person you're dealing with is actually a PSU student. There's no deposit mechanism, so there's no financial accountability. Condition isn't recorded before handover, so disputes over damage are purely word-against-word. And because the transactions happen in chat, there's no structured record — finding an old conversation about a rental from three months ago is basically impossible.

The Equipment Rental System this project proposes is an attempt to fix these problems with a proper digital platform. The basic idea is a web application exclusively for the PSU community where students and staff can list their equipment for rent and rent equipment from others, with the platform managing the deposit, the agreement, and the evidence record. Because both parties verify their PSU identity before using the system (through a KYC process that checks their student or staff ID), the community stays closed and trust is built in from the start.

What makes this different from just posting in a Facebook group is the escrow system. When a renter books an item, they pay a deposit and the rental fee upfront, but that money goes into the platform's escrow wallet — not directly to the owner. The owner only receives their payout after the rental is completed successfully. This gives both sides a reason to follow through properly: the renter doesn't lose their deposit for no reason, and the owner knows the payment is secured. If the item comes back damaged, the platform deducts the damage fee from the deposit based on what both parties agreed when signing the rental agreement.

The "Digital Handshake" is the other core mechanism. At both handover and return, both the owner and the renter independently open the platform on their devices, upload photos of the item, and sign a digital agreement using a canvas-based signature. The platform records the photos, signatures, and timestamps. This creates a tamper-evident evidence record. If someone claims the item was already damaged when it was returned, the photos from the handover and return are both on file and an admin can compare them directly.

Admin plays a specific role in this system as a neutral intermediary. They don't just moderate content — they hold the escrow, they enforce the rules, and in a dispute they function like a small-claims mediator. The admin can see every photo, every signature, every timestamp, and every message that's been exchanged. This "evidence vault" approach is what makes the system viable for handling real disputes between real people who have to see each other on campus.

From a practical standpoint, a system like this could save students real money. A DSLR camera rental for a weekend at ฿300 is much more accessible than buying one for ฿30,000. A costume rental at ฿150 for an event is far cheaper than buying something you'll wear once. Over time, a well-maintained rental platform creates a campus circular economy where people with equipment they don't constantly need can earn a small income from it, and people who need it temporarily can access it affordably.

1.2 Objectives

This project has three main objectives:

  1. Build a peer-to-peer rental platform exclusively for the PSU community. The system should support the full rental lifecycle — from listing and discovery through to booking, handover, rental period management, return, and settlement — within a closed community gated by KYC identity verification.
  2. Implement a safe escrow-based deposit and payment system. The platform should hold rental fees and security deposits in an escrow wallet, with automatic calculation and settlement of final amounts (including late fees and damage deductions) upon rental completion.
  3. Design a Digital Handshake system that creates a verifiable evidence record. Both pickup and return should be confirmed through a dual-party photo upload plus digital signature process, timestamped and stored so that admins have sufficient evidence to resolve disputes fairly.

1.3 Scope

In Scope (Project I)

  • User registration and login via email/password using NextAuth.js
  • KYC verification flow (student submits PSU ID + selfie, admin approves)
  • Equipment listing with photo upload, rental rate type (daily/monthly/yearly), deposit amount, and rental terms
  • Homepage browse and category/keyword search
  • Four-step rental checkout wizard (date selection, meetup scheduling, payment confirmation, agreement acceptance)
  • Rental request and owner approval/rejection workflow
  • Escrow wallet system: deposit held on approval, released on completion
  • Digital Handshake at pickup: both parties upload photos and confirm; renter signs digital agreement
  • Rental period tracking with countdown timer and automated overdue detection
  • Digital Handshake at return: both parties upload photos and confirm; owner rates condition and signs return agreement
  • Automated settlement on completion: damage fee deduction, deposit refund, owner payout
  • Rental contract PDF (downloadable at ACTIVE status)
  • Return receipt PDF (downloadable at COMPLETED status)
  • In-app notification system for status changes and reminders
  • Admin panel: KYC review, item moderation, order monitoring, basic dispute view
  • Role-based access control (RENTER / OWNER / ADMIN)
  • Soft-delete with grace period for item listings
  • AI-based item recommendations on homepage (category and price-match scoring)

Out of Scope (Planned for Project II)

  • Real payment gateway integration (PromptPay, Stripe). The current wallet system uses mock top-up only.
  • Native mobile application. The current system is web-only, though it is responsive for mobile browsers.
  • AI-powered photo damage comparison between pickup and return images
  • Integration with PSU official Single Sign-On (SSO) for automatic identity verification
  • Real-time in-app chat between renter and owner
  • Multi-campus expansion beyond Phuket Campus
  • Advanced analytics and financial reporting for administrators
  • Production deployment with monitoring, logging, and backup systems

1.4 Procedures

The development followed roughly seven phases, though in practice they overlapped quite a bit — especially design and development:

  1. Requirement Gathering. I surveyed around 30 PSU students from different faculties about their equipment needs, current borrowing habits, and what features they'd want in a rental platform. I also informally interviewed a few people who'd had bad experiences lending things informally. The results shaped the feature priorities significantly.
  2. System Design and Architecture Planning. Decided on the three-tier web architecture, chose the tech stack, and sketched out the main user flows using diagrams. This phase also included planning the rental lifecycle state machine, which turned out to be one of the most complex parts of the design.
  3. Database Schema Design. Designed the Prisma schema with all the main models: User, Item, RentalOrder, Wallet, WalletTransaction, KYCVerification, Notification, and more. Getting the relations right, especially around the escrow wallet and the rental order status history, took several iterations.
  4. Frontend Development. Built the UI components using Next.js 14 App Router with Tailwind CSS. Started with the main layout, homepage, and item cards, then moved to the more complex flows like the checkout wizard and the handshake screens.
  5. Backend Development. Implemented server actions and API routes for all the major operations: creating orders, processing escrow transactions, confirming handshakes, calculating settlements. This also included the rental lifecycle state machine logic.
  6. Integration Testing. Tested the complete rental flow end-to-end, checking edge cases like concurrent booking requests, expired orders, and the overdue detection cron job.
  7. User Testing with Peers. Had five PSU students test the prototype, observed them using the system, and collected feedback. Used the results to make final adjustments before writing up the report.

1.5 Expected Project Results

By the end of Project I, the prototype should demonstrate:

  • A working rental lifecycle from initial request all the way through return and settlement
  • A functional escrow wallet system that correctly calculates fees, deposits, and payouts
  • A dual-party Digital Handshake system with photo evidence and digital signature capture
  • Downloadable rental contract and return receipt PDFs
  • Admin tools for KYC review, content moderation, and order oversight
  • Positive usability feedback from at least five testers who successfully complete a full rental flow

1.6 Locations

Development and testing for this project took place entirely at Prince of Songkla University, Phuket Campus, within the College of Computing. All user testing was conducted with PSU students on campus. The system was run on local development machines during Project I; production deployment is planned for Project II.

I chose Next.js 14 mainly because of the App Router, which makes server-side rendering and server actions much easier to work with than a traditional separate frontend/backend split. The fact that server actions run on the server but are called from client components basically eliminates the need for a separate REST API for most operations, which simplified the codebase significantly.

Prisma was the ORM choice because the schema-first approach with auto-generated TypeScript types makes the database integration very safe. Whenever I changed the schema, the TypeScript types updated automatically and the compiler would flag any code that was accessing fields that no longer existed. This saved me from a few potential runtime bugs during development.

Tailwind CSS was a natural fit because it keeps the styling close to the component logic. For a project where I'm building and iterating quickly, not having to jump between CSS files and component files was a real time saver.
